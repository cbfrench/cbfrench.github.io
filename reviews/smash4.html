a smash hit

Super Smash Bros. for Wii U is the fourth game in the series that pits classic Nintendo characters against each other in friendly competition and fights to the death. The concept is simple enough, but it is in its execution that this game shines, making the strongest case of any Nintendo game to break down and get a Wii U. However, it definitely has its flaws to match its triumphs, so a deeper look into this game is required.

presentation

Super Smash Bros for Wii U is an excellent example of a great-looking game in an ugly package. While the graphics look great and the style the developers opted for fits well with the characters, the real tragedy is in the menus. The design concept has been carried over from the previous entry in the series, but the designers clearly prioritized form over function. The menus are rather unintuitive and unbalanced, with items like Smash Tour front and center, while settings and single player modes hidden under a vague "Games and More". This and other menu issues (the fact that the Online button on the front page is round while everything else is a jagged mess) hold the game back from being as clean as it could be, as it is graphically a substantial improvement over Brawl.

The music is another area that has substantially improved over the previous installment. Each one of the stages has multiple tracks that can be played, with the ability to pick and choose which ones play more often via the music menu. The tracks all represent their series well, and the various remixes show the amount of thought and care that was put in to making the many different styles of music fit into the game.

performance

The Smash series has always been extremely solid performance-wise. This game is no exception, sporting an astoundingly solid framerate of 60fps that seems to never drop even in technically demanding sections. This framerate, combined with the series' first foray into HD with a resolution of 1080p, make this game look and play remarkably well. This is made apparent in eight-player battles, where there is so much going on that it's surprising that the game doesn't falter in the slightest.

gameplay

Smash has always been about hitting opponents to rack up damage, using that increased damage to launch them off of the stage, and preventing their return to the stage to secure the kill. The game is marginally faster than Brawl, with faster run and fall speeds across the board, helping the game to be more offensive than its predecessor. This, combined with several quality-of-life improvements like the removal of random tripping and chain-grabbing, as well as the addition of ledge-trumping, only serve to improve the gameplay as a whole.

Ledge trumping in particular is a great new addition to the mechanics. In previous installments, a player could hold the ledge to force an offstage player to fall to their death, which is not the most exciting way to win the game. Now, if a player grabs the ledge while another is holding it, the player grabs it and forces the other player off, allowing for more offstage play and more interesting exchanges. Recoveries as a whole are stronger thanks to this technique, which allows each stock to last longer than in the previous games.

Of course, not all mechanics can be winners, and Smash 4 introduces a few losers into the formula. Rage is a mechanic that increases the power of attacks once you reach higher damage percentages. In theory, this would allow players with a lot of damage to make a comeback and defeat the player that's currently ahead. However, rage is not a static increase: it changes based on just how much damage you have taken, meaning that it never really behaves quite how you want or expect it to. Also, the increase in power lends itself to an increase in knockback dealt, meaning that once guaranteed combos or ways of finishing out a stock no longer work and the player is now forced to work harder for the kill.

However, perhaps no other mechanic change makes it harder to kill your opponent than the changes made to shielding. Shields are used to prevent damage from attacks and can be foiled by being grabbed. They are also used to dodge attacks with a directional input, which is where the majority of their problems come from. Having the ability to airdodge multiple times in the air is great, as it allows for more mindgames with your opponent. Rolling seems to have been improved as well, allowing you to escape from your opponent if you're in a bad position. Neither of these are bad when taken separately and sparingly, but the way the game works, oftentimes the optimal strategy for winning is to use these moves far too frequently. This style of play leads to more defensive matches, which are both less fun to watch and less fun to play.

That said, this is the Smash series; each game is still a ton of fun to play. Fighting against your friends and smashing them around remains just as satisfying as it's always been. While there may be a few missteps along the way, this game is a blast and was definitely worth picking up on the Wii U.

overall

Super Smash Bros. for Wii U has plenty of good ideas for improving the franchise and several good-intentioned ideas that didn't quite pan out. The game is still extremely polished and incredibly entertaining to play, and should absolutely be a purchase for anyone who calls themself a fan of Smash.
